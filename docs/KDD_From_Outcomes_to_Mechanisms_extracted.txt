From Outcomes to Mechanisms: A Benchmark for Diagnosing
LLM’s Mechanism Hallucination on Data Inference Externalities
Anonymous Author(s)
Abstract
Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed as decision-
making agents in environments with strategic interaction and
tightly coupled causal mechanisms. Yet most existing evaluations
remain outcome-centric, making it difficult to distinguish genuine
mechanism understanding from surface pattern matching. We pro-
pose a mechanism-understanding oriented benchmarking frame-
work for LLM-as-agent decision competence in computable eco-
nomic mechanisms. As a testbed, we instantiate a data-market game
withinference-driven privacy externalitieswhere cross-user infer-
ences create privacy externalities, suppressing equilibrium com-
pensation. We implement a reproducible rational-agent solver that
produces verifiable ground truth (equilibrium sharing sets, prices,
and welfare decomposition), and embed LLMs as users under an
isomorphic information structure and action space. Our diagnos-
tic metrics go beyond terminal outcome deviation by separating
outcome correctnessfrommechanism correctnessvia (i) strategic-
structure consistency, (ii) directional consistency under parameter
perturbations, and (iii) dynamic stability under mechanism feed-
back. Across 12 frontier models from the GPT, DeepSeek, and Qwen
families, we find that terminal metrics can substantially overesti-
mate mechanism understanding: several models attain near-zero
welfare deviation at the benchmark point yet violate mechanism-
implied comparative statics under correlation/noise shifts, exhibit-
ing a reproduciblemechanism hallucinationpattern: “looking right”
without internalizing the externality chain. Moreover, diagnostic
probes based on prompt explicitness and repeated feedback sepa-
rate prompt-dependent correctness from robust mechanism exe-
cution and reveal which errors are stably correctable. Overall, our
results position mechanism-oriented evaluation as a necessary com-
plement to outcome scoring and provide a portable blueprint for
building solver-verifiable, diagnostically rich benchmarks in other
economic mechanisms.
Keywords
LLM-as-agent; mechanism understanding;mechanism hallucina-
tion;inference externality; data markets; Stackelberg game

1 Introduction
In recent years, large language models (LLMs) have evolved from
“text generators” into executable decision-making agents and are
increasingly deployed in interactive environments with feedback
loops, including recommendation, pricing, negotiation, and policy
advisory [4, 16, 20, 32]. In these settings, success requires more
than plausible one-shot choices: an agent must track how actions
propagate through an economic mechanism—shaping information,
incentives, and other agents’ behavior—and how these effects feed
back into its own payoffs and risks [1, 24, 35]. Accordingly, evalua-
tion should treatmechanism understandingas a first-class criterion,
since it governs robustness under perturbations, distribution shift,
and long-horizon interaction [5, 22].
Privacy-externality data markets are a canonical testbed for
mechanism understanding. Individuals’ sharing decisions are strate-
gically coupled through informational spillovers and data exter-
nalities [2, 8, 10, 23], while platform inference and personalization
link “information–price–welfare” into a cross-stage causal chain
[7, 12, 18]. This coupling creates a sharp evaluation pitfall: a model
may match welfare or aggregate sharing at a benchmark point
while misunderstanding the mechanism objects that rational behav-
ior depends on (e.g., marginal leakage and diminishing marginal
information gains) [2]. Suchmechanism hallucination—looking right
on outcome metrics without internalizing the causal externality
chain—tends to surface as brittle reversals once correlation, noise,
or disclosure changes.
To detect such mechanism hallucination in LLMs, we propose a
mechanism-understanding-orientedbenchmarksystemand a gen-
eral methodology for constructing such systems in computable
economic mechanisms. Rather than assembling a one-off task suite,
our methodology turns a mechanism into a diagnosable evalua-
tion environment by (i) formalizing the externality pathway into
a small set of closed-loop structural objects, (ii) implementing a
theoretical solver that produces verifiable ground truth (equilib-
rium strategies, supporting payments, and welfare decomposition),
and (iii) defining a systematic approach to distinguishmechanism
correctnessfromoutcome correctness. We instantiate the recipe in
an inference-induced privacy-externality Stackelberg data market
[2], embedding LLM agents and the rational baseline under an iso-
morphic information structure and action space, and evaluating
against solver-backed ground truth to avoid manual labeling and
subjective preference [11, 28].
Our approach goes beyond terminal deviations (e.g., profit or
social surplus) and targets the signatures that mechanism hallu-
cination tends to evade: (i)strategic-structure consistency(e.g., set
alignment beyond aggregate rates), (ii)directional consistencyunder
perturbations that probe mechanism-implied comparative statics,
and (iii)process-level stabilityunder repeated interaction and feed-
back [5]. These three complementary diagnostics turn evaluation
into explanation: a prompt information-density ladder that locates

the environment changes. By separating outcome deviation from
structure-, direction-, and process-consistency, the benchmark sys-

than incidental prompt compliance. Overall, through operational-

portable benchmarking system, beyond a single benchmark instance,
to diagnose the LLM’s mechanism hallucination.
2 Related Work
Privacy Externalities in Data Markets.Our benchmark is grounded
in privacy economics, where correlated consumer data creates neg-
ative externalities via inference mechanisms [2, 10, 23]. The “social
dimension” of data lowers acquisition costs for intermediaries [7, 8],
while “privacy-choice externalities” in personalized markets can
actively harm non-sharers [6, 34]. Although theoretical literature
characterizes these market failures [3, 12, 18], emerging research
suggests autonomous agents may exacerbate such inefficiencies
[14, 36, 45]. We utilize these computable mechanisms as a rigorous
testbed for diagnosing the economic reasoning depth of LLMs.
LLMs as Decision Makers.LLMs are increasingly deployed as
decision makers [ 16], ranging from replicating social dynamics
[4, 32, 39] to macroeconomic forecasting [20, 26] and specific tasks
like trust games [43] or privacy management [38, 41]. While some
studies suggest advanced models can approximate rational behavior
[19, 27], substantial evidence highlights instability in their social
preferences [21, 25], psychological traits [33], and strategic robust-
ness [29, 40]. This unreliability in complex interactions necessitates
diagnostic evaluation beyond simple behavioral imitation.
Strategic Reasoning Benchmarks.Evaluation benchmarks have
shifted from general task frameworks likeAgentBench[ 28] to game-
theoretic suites such asGAMA-Bench[ 22],GTBench[ 11],Smart-
Play[ 42], andMAgIC[ 44], alongside behavioral analyses in classic
games [5, 9, 13, 15, 30]. However, these benchmarks predominantly
rely onoutcome-centricmetrics. Given that LLMs often lack in-
ternal causal models [1, 24, 35] or exhibit flawed chain-of-thought
reasoning despite correct outputs [17, 37], pure outcome evaluation
cannot distinguish genuine understanding from hallucination. Our
benchmark bridges this gap by explicitly identifying and measuring
themechanism consistencyagainst a rational solver.
3 A Benchmark Framework for Mechanism
Understanding under Inference Externalities
Figure 1: The Benchmark Framework. (Left) a computable
data market with inference externalities; (Middle) a dual-
agent protocol benchmarking LLM actions against a rational
solver via outcome and structural metrics; and (Right) a diag-
nostic suite comprising prompt ladders, sensitivity analysis,
and fictitious play to isolate mechanism hallucinations.
In canonical complex decision environments such as data markets
and platform governance, LLM decision capability should not be
evaluated solely by outcome-level metrics such as participation
rate, profit, and welfare. It should further be examined in terms of
whether the modelunderstands and follows the incentive structure
and comparative-statics regularities induced by the mechanism itself.
Accordingly, LLM decision capability should be characterized by
mechanism consistency: after entering a computable multi-agent
game as a strategic agent, whether the model-induced behavior
remains aligned with the equilibrium structure, binding constraints,
and comparative-statics directions implied by the mechanism.
Definition 1(Mechanism Consistency).Given a computable deci-
sion environment and its theoretical benchmark solution, a model
is said to exhibit better mechanism understanding in this environ-
ment if, under the same information structure, its induced strategic
structure is consistent with the theoretical benchmark on key struc-
tural objects, and it exhibits the same comparative statics under
changes in key parameters.
Based on this definition, we instantiate a mechanism-oriented
benchmark in the privacy-externality setting. A solver-backed ratio-
nal agent provides reproducible ground truth under the same infor-
mation structure and action space used by LLM-as-agent, enabling
direct comparison. Evaluation then separates outcome correctness
from mechanism correctness through a progressive diagnostic suite
(prompt ladder, parameter sensitivity, and mechanism-feedback
intervention), testing whether models converge under attributable
signals rather than merely matching outcomes.

two principles for scenario selection: a computable mechanism
ensures a generable ground truth, and a diagnosable evaluation
ensures localizable errors. Together, they make the benchmark both
evaluative and explanatory of mechanism understanding.
First,computable mechanism. The scenario must be repre-
sentable as anexecutable settlement-and-solution pipeline, so the
theoretical benchmark is not based on subjective annotation. Con-
cretely, the externality pathway is fully specified by structural
equations (or equivalent program rules), so that for any parameter
setting and action profile 𝑎: (i)agent-level payoffs are decompos-
able and computable(e.g., compensation, privacy loss, net utility,
platform information value, payment cost); (ii)equilibrium or best
response is solvable by deterministic procedures(e.g., enumeration,
optimization, fixed-point iteration), yielding reproducible 𝑎𝐸 and
𝑝𝐸; (iii)benchmark solutions are batch-generable across instances,
enabling statistical evaluation and sensitivity analysis. Thus, bench-
mark “correct answers” come from reproducible solver computation
under the same mechanism, not manual scoring or heuristics.
Second,diagnosable evaluation. The benchmark should not
only score proximity to theoretical outcomes, but also identify
where deviation occurs along the mechanism causal chain and
what structural misunderstanding it implies. Therefore, beyond
outcome metrics, the scenario must exposeintermediate mechanism
evidencedirectly comparable to solver benchmarks. This enables
detection of pseudo-understanding (correct outcomes but incor-
rect mechanisms) and anchors model differences to interpretable
mechanism dimensions.
Scenario Description: Data Sharing and Pricing under In-
ference Externality.Following the principles above, we use an
inference-externality data market as the canonical scenario. In this
data market, a platform offers take-it-or-leave-it, personalized com-
pensation for users’ data; users then decide in a one-shot game
whether to share. Sharing increases privacy loss via information
leakage, as user types are correlated and each user’s sharing im-
proves the platform’s inference about others, imposing negative
externalities on non-sharers. This feedback endogenously shapes
compensation: as others share more, a user’s baseline leakage rises
and the marginal privacy protection from “not sharing” falls, al-
lowing the platform to lower the minimum supporting price and
yielding the equilibrium pattern ofsuppressed compensation and
excessive sharing. This scenario serves as the common testbed for
static mechanism-consistency evaluation, prompt-ladder diagnosis,
sensitivity tests, and dynamic feedback interventions.
Why Data Inference Externality as the Scenario.As a canon-
ical evaluation setting, the inference-externality data market meets
bothcomputabilityanddiagnosability, with a clear externality chain
and sharply defined mechanism objects. First, externalities arise
from correlation and statistical inference: with correlated types, oth-
ers’ sharing increases the platform’s inference precision about an
individual, spilling privacy loss onto non-sharers; equilibrium shar-
ing sets and platform prices are solver-reproducible under explicit
settlement rules. Second, the setting exposes diagnosable structure:
“baseline” versus “marginal” leakage is decomposable, and whether
compensation covers marginal loss is directly testable. Moreover,
leakage submodularity yields sharp comparative statics: as more
others share, marginal privacy-protection gains from non-sharing
shrink, required compensation falls, and excessive sharing becomes
more likely. These regularities let us test whether an agent tracks
the externality feedback loop and comparative statics, beyond pro-
ducing superficially plausible share/non-share choices.

deepseek-r1 is smoother (0.88 ∼1.0) and reaches stable high consis-
tency in fewer rounds. Both are feedback-correctable, but differ in
convergence speed, trajectory smoothness, and stability retention.
The main implication is that,in mechanism-centric tasks,
final attainabilityandconvergence dynamicsare separable
capability dimensions. In particular,convergence dynamics
operationalize thestabilityof mechanism understanding:
models that reach high final Jaccard can still exhibit oscillations,
reversals, or slow settling, indicating that “correct” outcomes may
be supported by fragile internal reasoning—a dynamic signature
ofmechanism hallucination. Final Jaccard alone can therefore un-
derstate true model differences. Jointly evaluating FP trajectory
properties (speed, smoothness, and retention) and final consistency
distinguishes “correctable but unstable” from “correctable and sta-
ble” models, and turns mechanism hallucination from a pointwise
error into a measurable stability gap.
5 Conclusion
Our research concludes that evaluating LLM-as-agent decision ca-
pability should go beyond outcome proximity to a “correct answer”
but to directly test whether models follow the mechanism-implied
causal chain and comparative-statics logic. We thus propose amech-
anism understanding orientedbenchmark construction framework
includingscenario selection, agent implementation and mechanism
hallucination diagnostic suite. Following this framework, we choose
the inference-externality data market as a representative scenario.
Then we formalize the externality pathway as computable struc-
tural equations and a reproducible equilibrium solver to generate
ground truth, and embed LLM agents under the same information
structure and action space. A unified diagnostic suite then disen-
tanglesoutcome correctnessfromstructural consistency, directional
consistency, and dynamic stability.
Across 12 frontier models, we obtain four main findings. (i)
Outcome-only evaluation can overstate mechanism competence.
(ii) Models differ sharply in prompt dependence: some align stably
under low-information prompts, while others converge only when
externality, marginal leakage, and compensation logic are made
explicit, and weaker models may even regress when mechanism
language is strengthened. (iii) Outcome-level alignment can decou-
ple from mechanism-level directional consistency: certain models
achieve high set overlap at a single operating point yet violate
theoretical elasticity directions under perturbations in correlation,
noise, or privacy-preference distributions, yielding a reproducible
mechanism hallucinationpattern. (iv) Structured mechanism feed-
back in repeated interaction can turn one-shot errors into learnable
signals and substantially improve consistency for weaker models.
Overall, the proposed mechanism hallucination diagnosis offers a
practical path to characterize and improve LLM decision robustness,
by anchoring model differences to interpretable and reproducible
mechanism dimensions rather than terminal outcomes alone.

## Inference-Externality Mechanism
**Key concept**: Even if you do not share data, the platform can
infer your information from others'data.↩→
**Leaked information I_i(a)**:
- Given sharing set S, this is the increase in platform inference
precision about you↩→
- Computed via Bayesian updating: I_i(S) = sigma_i^2 - Var(X_i |
S)↩→
- **Core externality**: I_i depends on both your sharing decision
and others'sharing decisions↩→
**Your utility**:
- If you **share**: u_i = p_i - v_i * I_i(you share, others'
decisions)↩→
- If you **do not share**: u_i = 0 - v_i * I_i(you do not share,
others'decisions)↩→
**Key insights**:
- Not sharing still incurs **baseline leakage** (others'sharing
can leak your information)↩→
- True cost of sharing is **marginal leakage** = I_i(share) -
I_i(not share)↩→
- Compensation p_i is designed to cover your marginal privacy
loss↩→
## Rational-Expectation Decision Framework
Because you do not know others'exact choices, you should:
**1. Infer others'behavior from the distribution**:
- Users with lower v are more likely to share (lower privacy
cost)↩→
- Users with higher v are less likely to share (higher privacy
cost)↩→
- Your v = {v_i:.3f}, at {v_level} level
**2. Evaluate expected utility**:
- E[u_i | share] = E[p_i - v_i * I_i(1, a_{{-i}})]
- E[u_i | not share] = E[- v_i * I_i(0, a_{{-i}})]
**3. Use submodularity insight**:
- More sharers -> lower marginal information value from your own
sharing↩→
- Higher baseline leakage (more others sharing) -> lower marginal
leakage from your sharing↩→
**4. Choose best response**:
- If E[u_i | share] > E[u_i | not share], then share
- Otherwise, do not share

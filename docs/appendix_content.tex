\section{Convergence Proof for Finite Memory Fictitious Play}
\label{app:fp_proof}

In Section 3.3.3 (Test 3), we utilize Finite Memory Fictitious Play (FP) as a mechanism feedback intervention. To ensure the validity of this diagnostic test, we formally establish that the data market game with inference externalities constitutes a \textit{supermodular game}. Consequently, based on the theory of adaptive learning, FP dynamics (even with finite memory) are guaranteed to converge to the bounds of the set of pure Nash Equilibria.

\subsection{Game Theoretic Properties}

We adopt the framework of Acemoglu et al. [2], where user $i$'s utility given sharing profile $a$ and price vector $p$ is:
\begin{equation}
    u_i(a_i, a_{-i}, p) = p_i a_i - v_i \mathcal{I}_i(a)
\end{equation}
Here, $\mathcal{I}_i(a)$ denotes the information leakage about user $i$. The critical structural property of this game arises from the nature of information inference.

\begin{lemma}[Submodularity of Leakage and Increasing Differences]
\label{lemma:submodularity}
As established in \textbf{Lemma 1} of Acemoglu et al. [2], the leaked information function $\mathcal{I}_i(a)$ is monotone increasing and \textbf{submodular}. Specifically, for any user $i$ and any two opposing profiles $a_{-i}, a'_{-i} \in \{0,1\}^{n-1}$ such that $a'_{-i} \geq a_{-i}$ (component-wise):
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{I}_i(1, a_{-i}) - \mathcal{I}_i(0, a_{-i}) \geq \mathcal{I}_i(1, a'_{-i}) - \mathcal{I}_i(0, a'_{-i})
\end{equation}
This inequality implies that the marginal privacy loss from sharing, $\Delta \mathcal{I}_i(a_{-i}) \triangleq \mathcal{I}_i(1, a_{-i}) - \mathcal{I}_i(0, a_{-i})$, is non-increasing in the sharing decisions of others. Consequently, the user's incentive to share (net utility gain), defined as $p_i - v_i \Delta \mathcal{I}_i(a_{-i})$, satisfies the property of \textbf{Increasing Differences} in $(a_i, a_{-i})$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{theorem}[Supermodularity and Lattice Structure]
\label{thm:lattice}
Based on Lemma \ref{lemma:submodularity}, the user subgame for any fixed price vector $p$ is a \textbf{supermodular game}. According to Tarski's Fixed Point Theorem and \textbf{Lemma 2} of Acemoglu et al. [2], the set of pure strategy Nash Equilibria, denoted as $\mathcal{A}(p)$, is non-empty and forms a \textbf{complete lattice}.
\end{theorem}

This implies that while the equilibrium may not be unique (as shown in Example 1 of [2]), there always exist a "smallest" equilibrium $\underline{a}$ and a "largest" equilibrium $\overline{a}$ (in terms of the partial order of sharing sets).

\subsection{Convergence of Learning Dynamics}

We now address the convergence of Finite Memory Fictitious Play within this supermodular structure.

\begin{proposition}[Convergence to the Equilibrium Set]
In the data market game with inference externalities, the sequence of actions generated by Finite Memory Fictitious Play converges to the interval $[\underline{a}, \overline{a}]$, bounded by the smallest and largest pure Nash Equilibria of the game.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The proof relies on the general theory of adaptive learning in supermodular games developed by Milgrom and Roberts [1991].

\textbf{Step 1: Finite Memory FP as Adaptive Learning.}
Milgrom and Roberts define a sequence of plays $\{a^t\}$ as consistent with \textit{adaptive learning} if agents eventually choose strategies that are justifiable (undominated) responses to the opponents' past behavior over some history window. 
Finite Memory FP forms beliefs $\mu_i^t$ based strictly on the empirical frequency of the last $W$ rounds. As $t \to \infty$, if opponents' play stabilizes, the belief converges to the true distribution. Crucially, it assigns zero probability to strategies not played within the window, satisfying the "vanishing weight on distant past" condition required for adaptive forecasters. Thus, Finite Memory FP generates a sequence consistent with adaptive learning.

\textbf{Step 2: Convergence Theorem for Supermodular Games.}
\textbf{Theorem 5} in Milgrom and Roberts [1991] states that for any supermodular game, any sequence of plays consistent with adaptive learning will eventually lie within the bounds defined by the iterated elimination of strictly dominated strategies. For supermodular games, these bounds correspond exactly to the smallest ($\underline{a}$) and largest ($\overline{a}$) pure Nash Equilibria.

\textbf{Conclusion.}
Since the data market game is supermodular (Theorem \ref{thm:lattice}) and Finite Memory FP is an adaptive learning process (Step 1), the dynamics are theoretically guaranteed to converge to the set $[\underline{a}, \overline{a}]$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Uniqueness and Pointwise Convergence in Experiments]
While the general theory guarantees convergence to the set $[\underline{a}, \overline{a}]$, in our specific experimental instances (Section 4), the equilibrium is \textbf{unique} (i.e., $\underline{a} = \overline{a} = a^E$). This is ensured by three design choices in our simulation:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Generic Parameters:} Privacy valuations $v_i$ are drawn from a continuous uniform distribution, breaking parameter symmetry and making the platform's profit function almost surely have a unique global maximum.
    \item \textbf{Strict Preferences:} The pricing mechanism includes a strict margin $p_i = v_i \Delta \mathcal{I}_i + \epsilon$ (where $\epsilon > 0$), which eliminates user indifference and collapses the user-subgame lattice to a singleton.
    \item \textbf{Stackelberg Selection:} The platform explicitly optimizes over the equilibrium set to select the profit-maximizing outcome.
\end{enumerate}
Therefore, in our experiments, Finite Memory FP is guaranteed to exhibit \textbf{pointwise convergence} to the unique ground truth $a^E$.
\end{remark}
\section{Rational-Agent Solver Details for Scenario}
\label{app:rational_agent_solver}

This appendix provides the closed-form objects and the exact solver used to generate ground truth for Scenario~1.

\subsection{Gaussian posterior leakage in closed form}
Let $T=S(a)$ denote the set of sharing users and $S_a=(S_j)_{j\in T}$.
Under the Gaussian linear model with $S_i=X_i+Z_i$ and $Z_i\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$,
the leakage information in Eq.~\eqref{eq:leak_def} admits the closed form
\begin{equation}
I_i(a)=\Sigma_{i,T}\big(\Sigma_{T,T}+I\big)^{-1}\Sigma_{T,i},
\label{eq:leak_closed_form_app}
\end{equation}
where $\Sigma_{i,T}$ denotes the $i$-to-$T$ covariance row vector, $\Sigma_{T,T}$ the principal submatrix, and $I$ the identity of appropriate dimension.

\subsection{Baseline leakage, incremental leakage, and a closed-form relationship}
Baseline leakage and incremental leakage are defined in Eqs.~\eqref{eq:baseline_leak}--\eqref{eq:delta_def}.
Moreover, $\Delta I_i(\cdot)$ can be written as a closed-form function of $I_i^{0}(\cdot)$:
\begin{equation}
\Delta I_i(a_{-i})
=
\frac{\big(\sigma_i^2-I_i^{0}(a_{-i})\big)^2}{(\sigma_i^2+1)-I_i^{0}(a_{-i})},
\label{eq:deltaI_closed_form_app}
\end{equation}
which implies that as $I_i^{0}(a_{-i})$ increases, $\Delta I_i(a_{-i})$ decreases monotonically.

\subsection{Platform objective and exact Stackelberg selection}
In the baseline model, the platform's marginal value of information is normalized to 1 and the platform payoff is
\begin{equation}
U(a,p)=\sum_{i\in\mathcal{N}} I_i(a)\;-\;\sum_{i:a_i=1} p_i.
\label{eq:platform_obj_app}
\end{equation}
Under Stackelberg timing, the platform can implement any desired user equilibrium $a$ by setting $p=p^a$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:min_support_price}), and then selects the one maximizing $U(a,p^a)$.

For small $n$, we compute an exact ground truth via full enumeration:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Enumerate $a\in\{0,1\}^n$ (equivalently, enumerate sharing sets $T=S(a)$).
\item For each $a$, compute $I_i(a)$ by Eq.~\eqref{eq:leak_closed_form_app}, then compute $I_i^{0}(a_{-i})$ and $\Delta I_i(a_{-i})$ (via Eq.~\eqref{eq:delta_def} or Eq.~\eqref{eq:deltaI_closed_form_app}).
\item Construct $p^a$ by Eq.~\eqref{eq:min_support_price} and evaluate
\begin{equation}
U(a,p^a)=\sum_{i\in\mathcal{N}} I_i(a)\;-\;\sum_{i:a_i=1} v_i\Delta I_i(a_{-i}).
\label{eq:implementable_payoff_app}
\end{equation}
\item Select
\begin{equation}
a^{E}\in\arg\max_{a\in\{0,1\}^n} U(a,p^a),
\qquad p^{E}=p^{a^{E}},
\label{eq:eq_selection_app}
\end{equation}
with a fixed tie-breaking rule to ensure uniqueness and reproducibility.
\end{enumerate}

The outputs $(a^{E},p^{E})$, together with $\{I_i(a^{E}),I_i^{0}(a^{E}_{-i}),\Delta I_i(a^{E}_{-i})\}$, serve as verifiable ground truth for mechanism-level diagnostics.
